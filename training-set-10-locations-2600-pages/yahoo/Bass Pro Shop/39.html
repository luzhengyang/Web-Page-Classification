We'd sit mesmerized for a short time, and then venture off to the awesome food court to make a life decision over which place to eat that night. I mean there were dozens upon dozens of choices back then.. If I missed out on some A&W Root beer hot dogs because I decided to go to Sabarro's for a slice of pizza instead, it'd be a good week before I could rectify that mistake! This wasn't your cheap run of the mill course either.. it was hard and the plastic decorations gave you no mercy! Your ball hit the hard tire on the race car? I did, however, find a gorgeous sweater at Guess for a deeply discounted price.

    7/25/2011

    If there's a reason why greater Cincinnati does not need any more malls, this is it.  This mall (now with the oh so unique, creative name of "Cincinnati Mall") has likely been mostly vacant more than it has been mostly leased.

    Flash back to 1988, as Forest Fair Mall, when the single-story upper wing opened, with the second bigg's hypermarket opened, which introduced the concept of a supermarket and general merchandise discount store put together to the northern and western parts of greater Cincinnati.

    But the meat of the mall opened about a year later, as the largest shopping mall in the region, if not beyond. It was more than a mall - it had five department stores: the high-end Parisian, B. Altman, Bonwit Teller, Sakowitz, as well as the regional Dayton-based Elder-Beerman, a vast center court, Time Out on the Court, a BIG family entertainment center with a carousel, miniature golf, many video games and arcades, and an amazing ball maze machine that was a living work of art.  There were two food courts, a few restaurants, and the Super Saver Cinemas, which had the flashing neon lights behind glass block (epilepsy, anyone?)

    Being located in the middle-class areas of Forest Park and Fairfield, there weren't many upper-class shoppers in the vicinity, so many of the anchors and shops pulled out within a couple years, and the mall gained a reputation as a hangout for teens and gangs.  Also, worth note, it is right between two other malls along I-275 - Northgate, to the west, and Tri-County, to the east - which had just undergone a massive renovation by adding a second level.  Essentially, not only was this a bad place for your typical mall retailers, but also for unique, upscale ones.

    Developers set out to revamp the mall into "the Malls at Forest Fair" with some new renovations around 1993: the Bonwit Teller was turned into America Live!, an entertainement complex featuring night clubs, sports bar, and a restaurant - which itself didn't last too long.  Kohl's moved into the B. Altman space the next year, but Parisian pulled out not that much after.

    Then came the next resurgence, focusing on new anchor stores: Bass Pro Shops in the former Parisian, Burlington Coat Factory in the former nightclub space, Media Play, Saks off 5th, Steve & Barry's, and even a 10-screen first-run, stadium style Showcase Cinemas in addition to the Super Saver second-run cinema.  

    The mall then had its biggest remake yet, a thorough renovation throughout, when bought by the Mills, and converted to Cincinnati Mills - new life, mostly leased, and a positive attituude for a few more years, starting in 2004.  But again, slowly, the Mills fell behind, tenants left, and when the 2008 recession hit, this place pretty much became a Dead Mall.  Most of the specialty shops are gone now, Showcase was shuttered, and bigg's, after 20 years, decided not to renew its lease.  Its location seemed less visible compared to supercenters like Meijer and Walmart, both across from the mall.  

    Now known as simply Cincinnati Mall, this place still has several draws to it - Kohl's, Bass Pro, Burlington, Babies R Us, Danbarry cinemas, Red Lobster, Chipotle, and some other spots - why it's a 2 star rating for me.  But if I were a new developer, I'd explore redevelopment of much of the property, like office and residential uses.  There's not much an appetite for new retail in the region: Northgate has two vacant department store spots - and Tri-County, still viable, has its share of vacancies as well.

    Originally, this mall was called Forrest Fair.  My family used to head there when I was little to see the "cheapie" movies at the Showcase Cinemas inside the mall.  It used to be a great place to take little kids, too, as there was an entertainment complex with mini golf and laser tag (mini golf is still there), but now this mall has turned into the hangout for obnoxious teenagers due to the addition of 18+ up club Metropolis...not a place for young families, anymore.

It's sad.

None of those are enough to bring people all the way out to the Fairfield/Forest Park area, and familiar names like Game Stop and Finish Line can be found elsewhere. Throw in tacky stores like Everything Airbrush Plus, and that doesn't help matters either. Maybe the most defining thing is the sixth photograph I have posted of the big blue sign that's only half full of businesses.

    If you walk into Cincinnati Mills and are aware of the situation, you want to try to like the mall, but it's tough (especially with internal mall security riding on Segways). The mall is very nice, very spacious, and very clean (although with so few people shopping there it probably doesn't even need to be cleaned more than once a week), but there's no denying dozens of vacancies and several stores advertising sales off their entire merchandise. I feel almost obligated to give it a two-star rating, but without Bass Pro Shops, Cincinnati Mills would be lucky to muster more than a one-star rating.

Anyway, it felt so creepy walking down all of those empty corridors, the only sound coming from your footsteps and the shuddering of the blacked- out glass panes that were once stores. Oh yeah, and the odd sound of the generic music that sounds so weird over the quietness of the giant place.

    However, not everything is gone. The original stores that opened back in 1999 and 2000, Burlington Coat Factory, Kohls, Bass Pro Shops, and Baby's R Us are still in business and seem to be hanging on. Claires, Foot Locker, Purfumania, Payless Shoe Source, Susan G. Komen for the Cure, Denno's International Gifts, and OK Travel are also in business still. The food court only has two restaurants, Oyishi Japan and Gold Star Chili. The Bungy Zone bungy jumping is still there, but nobody really uses it anymore. Other stores that have closed down are Forever 21, Bath & Body Works Outlet, Auntie Ann's Pretzels, Tommy Hilfiger, Game Stop, and The Ohio National Guard. Believe me, there are many more businesses that have left that place than listed here.

    In all, I have to say my last  visit to Cincy Mills was the most devastating of all. It's so sad that the mall will never be the way it used to be ever again. The owners are talking about turning the defunct bigg's store into three ice rinks for figure skating and hockey. What is that? I can't believe that news, but it appears to be true. Other proposed renovations include a Candlewood Suites hotel, a new nightclub, a production studio, and a history museum. Ugh, I mean I obviously like history, but in a mall? It just doesn't mix.

    I'm truly sad because of all of this, and I will never view my hometown of Cincinnati the same ever again. R.I.P. Cincinnati Mills... :(

    Listed in The Don'ts

    Holy crap, what a surreal experience walking around this place was.

    Apparently when this place first opened it was called Forest Fair Mall.  When I moved to Cincinnati in 2005 the name had changed to Cincinnati Mills.  I remember driving by it a few times on I-275 and thinking to myself that seemed like a neat place and maybe I should swing by.  And I did, brought my wife and kids (only had two at the time).  The place was bustling, stores were packed in there, the food court was completely full, the kids play area had video screens and was officially sponsored by CET.  I thought this place was awesome.

    Sadly, I didn't really have the chance to go back very often.  In fact, I hadn't been here in...oh, probably three or so years.  Well, this past year (the first week of November in fact), my wife saw an ad from Bass Pro Shop saying that they were opening Santa's Village, or something along that lines, with an appearance from the Big Guy.  Now I wasn't all that keen on this (it was the first weekend of November for crying out loud, come on, one holiday at a time for frick's sake).  But the wife wanted to go, and she told the kids (we have four now) that Santa was going to be there and of course I wouldn't hear any end of it, so next thing I know we're in the van heading to the mall.

    First thing I noticed was that giant sign overlooking I-275 that said Cincinnati Mills, no longer said Cincinnati Mills.  Instead it said Cincinnati Mall.  Okay.  Guess some things have changed.  We pulled into the parking lot and noticed that it was fairly empty.  Somewhat odd considering it was a Saturday.  I could see the big, fenced-in area with all the big boats from Bass Pro Shop, so I figured everything was normal.

    Then we walked in...which resulted in the Holy Crap inference from before.
    You ever see that Dawn of the Living Dead movie from the seventies?  You know, the Living Dead zombie movie that takes place in the quasi-abandoned mall where there are a few walking dead moping around?  That would be the formerly Cincinnati Mills Mall, now named Cincinnati Mall, uh, Mall.  The place was a ghost town.  Almost every single store was empty.  And if that wasn't sad enough, there was an antique store with a little old man, who was obviously the owner, with no customers, and I just don't even know how to describe it, it was so depressing.  

    The food court had only two restaurants (one of which was the obligatory Chinese place).  The kids area was still there but all the screens and monitors had been disabled.  By the kids area was a giant divider that cut off a huge wing of the mall.  The lights were off until you reach the very end of the wing.  Spooky.  As you walked around the mall various doors leading to the back hallways that are usually off-limits to the public were just hanging wide open.  Other parts of the mall were blocked off with bits of string and signs that said "Not Open To The Public."   Retail space was just abandoned, some with signs saying they were no longer in business, others just empty with boxes and furniture laying around.  When we went there was a bunch of Christmas decorations strewn about.  Clearly what few people ran this mall planned on getting ready for the holiday season, although I can't imagine too many people are going to be spending a lot there.

    The only real big store that was there was Bass Pro Shop.  I guess the managers of the mall decided they needed to fill the place with something so they allowed them to throw a bunch of pontoon boats in the halls.  You could just hang out on these boats.  That was kind of cool.

    All in all, Cincinnati Mall is a pretty disheartening, depressing, and utterly creepy place to go shopping.  There isn't very much the place has to offer and as such, it really isn't worth the trip out there.

    Mahalo, and sad.
