Who you gonna call?

By: Bill Taylor Special to the Star, Published on Sat Oct 11 2008

Who you gonna call?

Because you will have to call someone to get rid of your old whatever-it-is if the tried-and-true neighbourhood self-recycling method lets you down.

That's where you put your old couch, dishwasher, computer, TV or what-have-you on the sidewalk and someone scoops it up and joyfully carries it home.

One person's broken microwave is another person's repair project.

You can, of course, donate your unwanted goods and chattels to charity.

The Ontario Federation of Cerebral Palsy, for instance, not only accepts "furniture, small appliances, knick-knacks'' and more, it'll come and pick them up. For more information, visit usedclothes.ca.

Goodwill, with depots all over the GTA, generally accepts electrical or battery-operated equipment that is less than 10-to-15 years old. The age depends on what the item is. Computers, for example, must have Pentium 2 or better.

Televisions, VCRs, DVD players, stereos and household furnishings, from bookshelves to bed frames, are all acceptable. As are reusable building materials and plumbing fixtures.

Large appliances, including fridges, freezers, stoves and washers and dryers, are likely to be turned down. And nothing will be accepted that is broken, doesn't meet current safety standards or is missing the essential operating instructions.

You'll have to take your stuff to Goodwill yourself and some donation centres can only accept certain things. For more details, the location of outlets and other information, visit www.goodwill.on.ca

Some big-box chains â Future Shop, for instance â have recycling facilities at certain stores. Buy a TV from them and you can leave your old one with them. But make sure you check first.

Municipalities all have their own rules about what they will, and won't, pick up and take away.

The City of Toronto is typical: Old computers can be taken to a solid-waste drop-off depot. Furniture, with a six-item limit on each collection day, can be put out with your regular garbage. Fridges, air conditioners, washing machines, dishwashers, stoves, etc. are collected by special trucks. You need to make an appointment for pickup.

For more information, visit toronto.ca/garbage/index.htm

ARCA Canada, a subsidiary of Appliance Recycling Centres of America, has a deal with the Ontario Power Authority to recycle "older, inefficient but working" fridges, freezers and air conditioners. For more information or to arrange pickup, call 1-877-797-9473.

The not-for-profit Electronic Recycling Association collects unwanted computers and electronic equipment either for recycling or donation to schools, libraries, charities and community organizations. The ERA also guarantees to remove all data from your hard drive first. Visit era.ca.

Finally, there are those outfits that are forever leaving flyers â easily recycled, fortunately â at your home.

The biggest is 1-800-GOT-JUNK?

The company will clean out your garage, or wherever your junk is stored, truck it away and leave the area neat and clean. But it'll cost you and you'll need to get someone out to give you a price.

"We are not a salvaging company or charity," according to the website. "We are charged at the dump, just as you would be."

That said, the company also promises, "We do our best to recycle as much junk as we can."

The Toronto franchise has raised thousands of dollars for charity by donating truck loads of "junk" in usable condition.
