The "Morel" Of The Story Is....

Posted by Cincinnati Ohio on Monday, February 11, 2013

On the south side of the hill, when the oak tree’s new leaves are about the size of a mouse’s ear, it’s time to go morel mushroom hunting. The morel’s like to grow under fruit-bearing or nut-bearing trees.  They thrive in the decomposing fruits, nuts and berries under these trees, which include beech, apple, oak and hickory among others.

Depending on your region, these delicious mushrooms can be found between April and mid-May, especially after a good spring rain. Air temperature, ground temperature and rain levels impact the growing cycle of the morel and will influence when and where you can find them.  One fact for sure is that they grow in the same locations year after year so once you find that honey pot spot you should be able to return there and collect your bounty for years to come. Put the coordinates in your GPS and save this location along with your other favorite spots.

First time hunters beware, you should take your first hunting expedition with someone who knows what a good morel looks like. There are several types of morels, some edible and others poisonous. There are also other fungi that the woods will produce, so educate yourself if you don’t have a seasoned pro. There are many websites and books that have been written to help you identify the edible mushrooms and will aid you in your hunt this season.

Morel mushrooms are very sought after and rare, so you want to properly collect these delectable treats. These fungi emit thousands of spores but very few find the exact conditions to grow and thrive. Use a mesh bag to collect them so that as you walk the spores will be emitted and drop through the holes and have a chance to grow where they land.

Some of my fondest memories of my youth are my father taking me out on damp spring mornings and collecting morels. We would fill up our bags and then take them back to the house where my mother would prepare them. First she would soak them in salt water for a couple hours to clean them and get out the small insects that have taken up habitat in them. Then she would blot them dry, dip them in egg, roll them in flour with salt and pepper to taste, drop them in oil for several minutes, or until they begin to float and place them on a paper towel to drain. Use a favorite dipping condiment, or eat them as is. Bon Appetit !!!

(Warning: some mushrooms are poisonous, even deadly. Use extreme caution when hunting mushrooms for consumption. The information in this article does not offer sufficient information for a safe mushroom hunt, because some mushrooms are poisonous, even deadly, so use extreme caution.)

Did You Know?....Geocaching In Our Area

Posted by Cincinnati Ohio on Friday, February 8, 2013

Back in the infancy of our blogsite, we asked you the question, “Are you a thing-finder?” referring to geocaching and how to get involved. Here is a link to that blog. So today’s question is “Do you have any idea how many things there are to find in the area?” I am sure for most of you the answer will astound you.

Below is a map of the area immediately around our store, with each of the blocks you see representing a cache. As of this writing there are nearly 4400 caches within 25 miles of the store, and that number grows everyday as geocaching gets more popular. Some of the locations are out in the open while others are more hidden. That’s the fun of it, each one is unique unto itself.


So what are you waiting for? Go to geocaching.com and pick out some local spots to get started. Dust off your GPS and pull on your boots and get “thing-finding”. When you get done, be sure to go back to geocaching.com and write about your adventure, whether you found them or not. Be sure not to give away the details of the location and have fun with it :)
