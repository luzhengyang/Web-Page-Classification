Barrie fire department hosting events around the city 3

By Cheryl Browne, Barrie Examiner

Friday, October 3, 2014 7:58:17 EDT PM

During the 2014 Fire Prevention Week, Oct. 4-11, the Barrie Fire and Emergency Services will be holding a number of events throughout the week to raise public awareness about testing smoke alarms. Above, Deputy Fire Chief of Prevention Jeff Weber and Public Fire and Life Safety Officer Samantha Hoffmann look at several different types of smoke alarms. MARK WANZEL/PHOTO

Judith Myddelton had just returned home from work when she heard a distant fire alarm sound.

Looking out the window, the fire prevention officer with Barrie Fire & Emergency Service saw a mother and daughter holding hands as they walked calmly towards a tree on the boulevard. Behind them, an older daughter came next, followed by her father.

“When they all got to the tree, they high-fived,” Myddelton said. “Dad had been cooking and the smoke alarm had gone off, so they decided it was a great time to practise their fire safety drill.”

Myddelton said she ran over to praise their actions, adding she hopes other families will be convinced to do the same during Fire Prevention Week, Oct. 4 through to Oct. 11.

As we add more electrical appliances to our homes, Samantha Hoffmann also with the Barrie Fire department, said we need to add more smoke alarms.

“We put so much in our bedrooms now, from computers to straightening or curling irons and a TV, if anything should malfunction you need to warn people quickly. But that's not always easy, because now homes have a teenager's room in the basement, have people with limited mobility or small children, you need to have enough alarms to warn everyone at the same time,” Hoffmann said.

In the last dozen years, efforts to improve smoke alarm safety from both the Ontario Building Code and Ontario Fire Code (OFC) have meant tightening regulations regarding installations in new buildings.

Smoke alarms now have to be hardwired (not battery-operated) and inter-connected to relay information to each other.

Older homes can still have battery-operated smoke alarms, but they should be tested every year and batteries should be replaced when Daylight Saving Time ends this year on Nov. 2.

After the CFB Borden fire in 2005 when five-year-old Brandon Fuller died – he woke up his family and they escaped, but he didn't – the OFC was changed. It became law that all homes have working smoke alarms on every level near bedrooms.

A fire earlier this year also prompted changes after a family of four in Sharon died when a fire started in the front foyer of their home. The lower-floor alarm sounded but couldn't transfer the warning to the upstairs alarm because it had been changed to a battery model and the battery had died.

New regulations mandating hardwired sets must only be refitted with hardwired units will be coming into effect later this year.

“You've got to replace like with like,” said Barrie's new Deputy Fire Chief of Fire Prevention Jeff Weber.

On the job less than a week, Weber hails from Burlington where he helped create a series of testing smoke alarm videos that are available on YouTube.

As the new 2014 OFC becomes law, Weber said another significant change will be tenants now have a responsibility to let landlords know if their smoke alarms aren't functioning.

“That's a diametric change,” he said, adding previously landlords' hands were tied if tenants removed fire alarms because landlord's were held solely liable.

However, new products on the market are making fire safety more reliable. Manufacturers are now selling smoke alarms that come with a 10-year lithium battery, as well as room-specific alarms, so kitchen sensors won't over-react when the toaster does.

FIRE ALARM RECALL

A smoke alarm product recall from Kidde Canada was announced Friday. The recall concerns smoke alarms and combination smoke and carbon monoxide alarms, sold between January and May 2014.

Kidde said model KN-COSMIBACA, iL2010S-CO-CA and iL2010S-CA may not respond correctly if a once-per minute sensor test occurs during a power outage. For more information, contact Kidde Canada at 1-888-784-2323.

FIRE PREVENTION WEEK

 

To celebrate Fire Prevention Week, Barrie Fire Service is hosting several events including:

October 4

Fire Safety Day at Lowe's

71 Bryne Drive – Noon – 2 p.m. Free BBQ, fire truck and bouncy fire truck for the kids

Home Depot Fire Safety Day

10 Barrieview Drive

10 a.m. - 2 p.m.

Fire truck on display and fire safety crafts with firefighters for the kids

October 5

Fire Station #1 Open House

155 Dunlop Street West

10 a.m. - 2 p.m.

Chief John Lynn invites residents to tour the station. Activities for all ages, including bouncy fire truck, mini-combat challenge, face painting, movies and popcorn.

October 6

New Sunnidale Playground Opening

10 a.m. Sunnidale Park near Dorian Parker Centre

Help celebrate the new firefighter-themed playground at the Every Kid's a Hero-Adventure Playground fully accessible park.

October 8

11th annual Home Escape Night & Street Party

Win a street party by voting for your ward in the Facebook challenge; visit www.facebook.com/cityofbarrie to cast your vote.

October 9

Domino's Pizza Partnership

6 p.m. - 8 p.m.

While delivering pizzas for Domino's Pizza, once Barrie firefighters determine you have working fire alarms, the pizza is free.
