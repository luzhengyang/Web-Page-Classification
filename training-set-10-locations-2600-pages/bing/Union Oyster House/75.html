    Union Oyster House was one of the first restaurants I ate at in Boston. The main reason I wanted to check it out was the history. UOH is listed as the oldest restaurant in the US.

    To be honest, the food was not as good as I was expecting. The ambiance and location are both great and being able to say you've eaten at the oldest restaurant in america is pretty cool. If you're seated on the second floor you really feel like you're in an old tavern. However, I was seated in the renovated area which came off much more modern.

    I ordered a cup of the clam chowder and for my main course I had the lobster risotto. The clam chowder didn't blow away my taste buds, quite honestly I've had better chowder in California. The lobster risotto was creamy and quite bland. I picked out all the lobster pieces and was left with a heavy cream sauce and rice.

    On the plus side, everyone gets a slice of corn bread which ended up being the best thing I ate.

    While the food wasn't that great I have taken visitors back for the experience. However, we usually just go for drinks and move on to a more affordable restaurant.

I partially resolved the situation by moving the contents of the cup to the saucer underneath.     The eating of mussels requires the use of one hand and a fork in the other hand - this can be a little messy. And yet, no wet nap was offered.

    After paying the bill, when I courteously offered my constructive criticism to the manager, no apology was offered - only excuses or explanations of "that's the way we do it."

    I'm transitioning from being a tourist of Boston to a resident in the coming months, but had never eaten at Union Oyster House until this past weekend. I think my expectations were set too high, and had I read the reviews, I would have guessed that this would not be the "ideal" restaurant to enjoy my last seafood dinner during vacation.

    Wait time: 30 minutes on a Sunday for dinner service (~4:00PM)

    Service: generally most of the staff seemed disinterested. When we arrived, the downstairs hostess had her head down and appeared to be asleep. It took two tries to wake her. Our waitress was very polite, brought everything out in a timely fashion, and apologized when we got up to find her and ask for lemons. As a general statement, I was a bit surprised that most of the staff was not dressed in a way that would reflect their menu prices. To me, high price = high class = professional uniform and appearance.

    Ambiance: olde world tavern feel with yellow lighting (upstairs). A bit hard for the eyes to adjust to, but not troublesome. The upstairs was quite loud between guests walking through, the staff talking amongst themselves, cleaning tables, etc. I was hoping for a more intimate dinner experience, but this was not exactly it. Downstairs, the oyster bar was consistently packed, with both diners and gawkers, with lots of picture-taking. If you're like me, there's nothing comfortable about tourists cramming in around you, taking pictures of you slurping oysters.

    Food: complimentary cornbread with Cabot butter served for starters was very tasty. I ordered the Lazy Man's Lobster (a special) and my fiancée (!!!) ordered shrimp ravioli. My dish, akin to a casserole, was big hearty chunks of lobster meat baked with cream, sherry and breadcrumbs in a dish; it also came with a side, I opted for new potatoes. Jamie's dish had both shrimp in the ravioli and was also garnished with whole shrimp and vegetables. The lobster casserole was VERY rich and buttery, I ate about 1/3 of it before realizing I really needed some acidity to cut through the richness (hence the lemons). In my opinion, their heavy use of Ritz-like breadcrumbs took away from delicious, velvety lobster. Thankfully, there was a lot of good quality, perfectly cooked lobster, which helped with the price sting.

    Price: Dinner for two without drinks (before tip): $68. If the ambiance had matched the plate price, I would be happy to return to UOH. However, this restaurant seems more suited to the occasional "nice dinner" to treat your relatives visiting from out-of-state.

    Listed in My Food Journal 2014.

    Great restaurant along the Freedom Trail.  This used to be my favorite restaurant to go for seafood in Boston.  Yes, this place is old...188 years, to be exact!!  It's still an amazing place, though, that's definitely worth going at least once.

    The ambiance of this place is truly one-of-a-kind; has that historic aura with all dark wooden flooring and walls.  The place was very dim, almost too dark, and it has that dungeon-y feel to it...not so great for a first date (cause it's too dark & can be a bit creepy) but great to bring an out-of-town visitor for a genuine Boston dining experience.

    Things I love:
    *Seafood platter ($30): Yay for all deep fried seafood (deep fried shrimp,clams, scallops,calamari, oysters and filet of fish)!  Generously portioned and the seafood fried to perfect crunchiness...delicious!  They also gave us a side of corn bread with every order which tasted amazing!

    *Shore Dinner: More like a full course meal with an appetizer to start with (Clam chowdah; so rich and creamy!!!) then moved on to their main entree: A steamed lobster, bag of mussels, corn, potatoes....noms noms!

    And lastly, for dessert at the end: a choice of gingerbread or Indian pudding...I went with the Indian pudding, but honestly didn't like it as much.  Thought there were too much spices going on inside that tiny dessert.

    Prices can be a bit on the high end.  After all, you are eating in a Tourist hot spot (Faneuil hall).

    Now that I'm writing this review it makes me miss Boston...T.T  Well. it was a great experience to have dined at the oldest restaurant in Boston.  All in All, amazing food in an amazing historical building.  Thank you UOH...til we meet again...

    Until this past Monday, I had never visited Boston. When my family and I arrived in the city, it was late in the evening and we had little time to chose a place to eat. With a member of our party in a wheelchair, our options were somewhat limited. The Union Oyster House caught my eye, because it was visually charming and not full of such a boisterous crowd like many restaurants we'd passed. So we decided to look into eating at the restaurant and quickly found that the staff was one-of-a-kind. An outwardly friendly member of the staff rushed to the entrance to put a ramp over the stairs- so that a wheelchair could enter the restaurant with ease.

    We were seated in a cozy room full of décor reminiscent of Boston's history. Our waitress was nothing but friendly, and we felt very comfortable in the restaurant. As someone who is not a HUGE seafood fan, I decided to try their pan-seared chicken breast as its description was enticing. Three of my dining partners ordered the broiled scrod, while one chose the pan-seared haddock. When the meals came out, the portions seemed slightly small... but we soon learned that they were perfectly sized. My meal was the best "non-seafood" meal I'd ever eaten at a primarily seafood serving restaurant. All who'd ordered the scrod were entirely pleased- and the haddock was deemed delicious.

    We were all so satisfied that we came back the next day! That evening I did order fish & chips and could agree that fish is something this restaurant truly does right. Overall, I have not a single complaint for the Union Oyster House and recommend it to everyone. Delicious food, charming ambience, wonderful staff.
