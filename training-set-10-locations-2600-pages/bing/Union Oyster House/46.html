Union Oyster House, Boston

 Follow

Reviews

Write a review

A bowl of good old clam chowder in New England


We all know that New England is famous for its clam chowder. So, when I found myself in Boston with three days to spare, I made it a mission to sample the best clam chowder in town. Consulting the opinions of a few people and my guide book, all pointed me to the direction of Union Oyster House.

Being one of the oldest-running restaurants in the United States of America, Union Oyster House is so much more than just an old seafood place. Opened in 1826, the restaurant serves as the backdrop for some of the most amazing stories in the histories of America.

Don’t worry, I’m not going into a spiel about who and who went there and who proposed to who and what was first invented there. The anecdotes of the big-wigs are presented on the wall (we’re talking about presidents and kings here.) But still, there's no doubt that history is part of the charm. The restaurant is housed in an charming old brick building that sits subtly on the freedom trail, a red-marked trail that walks you throughout the landmarks of Boston. If not for the picture-taking Chinese tourists, I probably would have missed it. As soon as I stepped in the restaurant, I immediately had the impression that I was just zapped back to the 1800s.

The décor is quaint and thank goodness they did not go overboard to the point of kitsch. I’m wondering if any renovation had ever been carried out on the establishment before, because I was impressed by how they managed to maintain the antique-y ambiance without looking a slightest bit run-down. The lights and the wooden floor made the whole place feel so warm, which is a huge contrast to outside. (Apparently, early September in Boston can be quite chilly.) On the ground floor, there is an oyster bar, a gift shop (of course), and a huge tank for the lobsters and all.

We were soon led to upstairs by a lovely gentleman who told us we were very lucky since we didn’t make a reservation beforehand and he was right. The place was packed with people, mostly tourists. The service was efficient and the our waitress was really helpful and patient with us. My friend ordered the specialty of the house- oysters (duh) while I went for the clam chowder and lobster. The lobster was fresh-caught, succulent and sweet. The clam chowder was a classic, creamy, rich in texture. Watching my friend (a first-timer) slurping up the raw oyster also adds to part of the fun. Overall, we couldn’t have been happier we went there.

How to get there:
If you’re taking the T(subway, that is), get off at North Station.
It’s about 10 minute walk from the station, near Faneuil Hall Marketplace and Quincy Market(Where I also tried the clam chowder at one of the food stalls, I’m not exaggerating, unless you like your clam chowder with half bottle of salt in it, otherwise, stay away. )

Address: 41 Union St., Boston, MA
Phone: 617-227-2750 [more ]

America's oldest restaurant


The famous Oyster House has been serving seafood since 1826, thereby making it the oldest restaurant in the United States. The establishment has been a Boston favorite for nearly its entire existence. It is also a particularly popular place for local politicians. The Kennedy family is among the restaurant’s famous regular visitors. In fact, a booth upstairs was renamed in honor of its well known frequenter, John F Kennedy. Also, John Kerry is known to be a big fan of their clam chowder. While the meals are a little more expensive than the average restaurant, to me the history is reason enough to visit. I’ve also stopped by just to enjoy the atmosphere and drink a beer or two. [more ]
