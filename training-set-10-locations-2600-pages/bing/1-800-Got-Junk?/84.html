One man's junk is another man's business

Published on July 24, 2000
  • Share 0
  • inShare0
  • 2 Comment
  • Send to a friend
  • Print

BY BRIAN SALISBURY ottawa business journal

When Pierrette Raymond has a gut feeling, she acts on it. How else can you explain her decision to pack up her family of four and move to Ottawa to open up a junk removal business?

"All of my family in Timmins thinks I'm crazy, but I've got a gut feeling going on here. I know this will be successful," says the 31-year-old risk taker.

She and her husband, Luc, are the Ottawa-area franchisees of 1-800-GOT-JUNK, a Vancouver-based business that recently began franchising. The multimillion-dollar company started in 1989 when Brian Scudamore purchased a $700 pick-up truck and started hauling junk too big for the curbside to fund his university education. Since then, he has reached the $1-million mark, while receiving a throng of media attention, including coverage in Maclean's, USA Today, The Wall Street Journal and Success Magazine.

There are other franchises in Toronto, Calgary and Edmonton and Scudamore is shifting his focus to larger American markets.

Before purchasing a franchise, Pierrette owned her own successful home-based business selling kitchen tools, while Luc worked as a copper miner at the Kidd Creek Met Site in Timmins. The couple has two children, aged three and six.

"Six months ago if you told me that I'd be this excited about junk I would have said you were crazy. Now, I want to be the FedEx of junk," says Pierrette, who takes care of sales and marketing. Luc does the hands-on work, riding the truck and hauling the junk. "All we were really looking for was a way to replace Luc's income from the mine. I was making enough to support both of us, so we could afford him quitting the mine and looking for another job."

The other job turned out to be junk hauling, which Pierrette found to be so lucrative that she sold her kitchen tool business and joined forces with her husband.

Paul Guy knows about the potential of Raymond's franchise. He represents the 1-800-GOT-JUNK name in Toronto and booked more than $250,000 in sales in 1999 using two trucks. He now has six trucks on the road with plans to add an additional two in the near future. "The fact is, everyone has junk. 1-800-GOT-JUNK has become the brand name in markets it has penetrated. The system works," says Guy.

With visions of replicating Guy's success, Pierrette's transition from kitchen tool sales to junk hauling has been dramatically quick. On the way home from attending a franchisee conference in Toronto in late March, she and Luc stumbled upon an ad for 1-800-GOT-JUNK in Small Business Magazine. Pierrette placed a call to the company that night and the wheels started turning.

The company asked the Raymonds to do some market research in Ottawa, so they got out the yellow pages and started calling. Pierrette says there wasn't much competition in the nation's capital and the mom and pop operations that did haul junk had poor customer service. (1-800-GOT-JUNK has a call centre that fields inquiries and books jobs for its franchisees.)

"It was like, Wow, nobody is doing this. We want in. We want in now,'" says Pierrette.

From there, the couple submitted their financial information to the company and spent April looking for financing. With banks being leery of bankrolling the project a $60,000 proposition the Raymonds decided to use Luc's RRSPs.

The final step was a face-to-face interview in Vancouver with the 1-800-GOT-JUNK executive. The couple flew to Vancouver on May 25 to seal the deal. Mission accomplished.

However, there was one small detail: selling their house in the saturated Timmins market and buying a house in the congested Ottawa market. "If we had known what we were up against we might have stopped in our tracks, but we were so motivated to make this work. I was just loving the ride," says Pierrette.

The Raymonds put their house up for sale the first week of June. Amazingly, the couple was able to sell their Timmins home and buy a house in Ottawa that very same week.

"Everything just fell into place for us. We were floored by the whole experience," says Pierrette.

Now, the couple is hitting the streets to extol the virtues of junk hauling. So far, the response has been positive, says Pierrette. "I'm bouncing off the walls. We just started two days ago (July 17) and we've already got two referrals."

Pierrette is forecasting revenues of $200,000 in the Ottawa franchise's first year with 58 per cent of that amount being profit. The Raymonds currently own one truck, but hope to add their second by the end of August.
