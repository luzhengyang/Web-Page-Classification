    About your stay

    Once the tropical playground for the likes of Frank Sinatra, Dean Martin and Elvis Presley, Miami Beach’s legendary Fontainebleau was reborn in 2008 after a $1 billion makeover that raised destination resort luxury to dizzying new heights. Beyond the expansive poolscape and a pristine slice of sand, the 20-acre oceanfront hotel packs in 12 restaurants, including Alfred Portale’s Gotham Steak and Scott Conant’s Scarpetta, and LIV, SoBe’s hottest nightclub. Topping it all off is the 40,000-square-foot Lapis Spa overlooking the ocean and featuring a novel-thick menu of treatments.

    What we love

    • Morris Lapidus’s 1954 designs in the original Fontainebleau building; the Florida chapter of the American Institute of Architects recently named it Florida’s best building in the last 100 years
    • Sprawling pool complex lined with cabanas
    • The 40,000-square-foot Lapis Spa with 30 treatment rooms
    • Twelve on-site restaurants have all eaters covered, from steak lovers to vegetarians

    What to know

    • Stay two nights or more and receive a food and beverage credit if you select the Special Promo rate at checkout; travel through April 30, 2014
    • A $20 resort fee per night will be collected by Jetsetter at checkout
    • The hotel is sexy and sceney; while families do stay here, but it's not for every family

    The Review

    What’s the secret to Fontainebleau’s success as Miami’s premier destination resort? Look no further than the title of the designer’s autobiography: Too Much is Never Enough.


    The spacious lobby's bowtie floor is a tribute to architect Morris Lapidus's original design.     That was the design philosophy of legendary architect Morris Lapidus, who tricked out Fontainebleau as a glitzy ode to opulence when it opened in 1954. Critics derided the 11-story tower that embraced the ocean in a gentle curve, with its 17,000-square-foot, antique-filled lobby and famed “Stairway to Nowhere,” which allowed fashionistas and celebs to make grand appearances from above. But 1960s A-Listers and pioneering jetsetters ate it up, making a stay at Fontainebleau a must for everybody from the mayor of Fontainebleau, France, to The Beatles.

    Fast-forward five decades and Fontainebleau is back at the top of its game, emerging from a three-year, $1 billion reinvention in 2008 to embrace Lapidus’s more-is-more philosophy in every aspect, from dining to nightlife to service. Fontainebleau leads the charge in SoBe's foodie scene with gaudy Gotham Steak by Michelin-stared chef Alfred Portale, rustic Scarpetta by James Beard-recognized chef Scott Conant and the first U.S. outpost of Hakkasan, fusing Cantonese and Continental fare. Uber-exclusive LIV and iconic Bleau Bar hold down the hotel’s claim to the beach’s nightlife hub, and if you party too hard, you can revive at the 40,000-square-foot Lapis Spa. Or maybe a cabana at the sprawling pool complex is required? Each furnished Premium cabana accommodates six guests, is equipped with a 32-inch flatscreen and includes the full attention of a dedicated butler.

    Fontainebleau’s 1,504 guestrooms and suites are spread over four towers; there are 846 rooms in the two original Lapidus buildings and 658 suites in two all-suite towers. All are outfitted in shades of white, with ivory leather headboards and cream chairs and couches complimented by pops of sea green and blue. And all have marble bathrooms, flat-screen TVs, iPod docking sound systems and iMac computers connecting guests to all the hotel’s services.

    Suites are decadent affairs, the best spreading out over more than 800 square feet and looking out over the ocean from towering heights. But I loved my smallish Oceanfront Balcony room in Lapidus’s landmark Chateau tower. The balconies on this room type overlook the pool and, further afield, the Atlantic. But they also look in on each other ever so slightly thanks to the building’s leisurely curve. The architect famously noted that when you create a grand stage, everyone who enters will play their part, and watching vacationers sip cocktails, chat, dance and strut on their oceanfront balconies, it’s clear that Lapidus’s predictions for Fontainebleau are as prescient as ever.

    How to get there

    Flights are available to Miami International Airport (MIA) from major U.S. cities on a variety of carriers. Transfers to/from MIA, about seven miles away, are available for an additional cost depending on the number of guests, and can be arranged through the property. Taxis to/from MIA are approximately $32, each way. If you’re renting a car for the transfer, valet parking is available for $36 plus tax, per day.

    What to do

    • Feast. Once you get your fill of Fontainebleau’s delish restaurants, head to Yardbird: Southern Table & Bar. The place is packed with locals filling up on fried chicken, ribs, grits and biscuits — all washed down with a mint julep or a shot of bourbon.
    • Shop. To follow the local look, pick up threads and sky-high heels fresh from the runway at designer boutique Alchemist. There are two outposts, one at the beach end of Lincoln Road and a second at the top of the architectural parking lot, 1111 Lincoln Road.
    • Jam. The New World Center is SoBe’s Frank Gehry–designed venue for a show or a blend of classical and electronica at “Late Night at the New World Symphony.”
    • Walk. Follow Miami Beach’s oceanfront path all the way down to South Pointe Park, the picturesque end of South Beach. It’s the perfect place to take in the sunset, watch the departing cruise ships, or ogle the impossibly good-looking locals walking their dogs.
