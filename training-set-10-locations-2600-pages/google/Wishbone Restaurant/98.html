Wishbone review: Likable, but inconsistent
gallery_overlay_close|article-gallery-5390416|article-gallery-5390416|0
Back to Gallery
gallery_thumbs_close|article-gallery-5390416|article-gallery-5390416|0

Sometimes, smitten by its charming decor, endearing staff and enticingly written menus, you really want to love a restaurant. Yet sometimes your crush can lead you to excuse stumbles, until finally the glow wears off and you have to admit: One of your favorite places might just be pretty good.

That's how I feel about Petaluma's Wishbone. The 36-seat restaurant, which opened in November, is darling in a bohemian way, beginning with the six-seat counter fronting the partially open kitchen.

The stools are covered in random worn fabrics to go with the mismatched wood and schoolroom-style furniture scattered around the room. The Beatles play on a retro turntable, and servers dressed in jeans and blue workman aprons offer you a deck of cards if you feel like playing.

The American home-style cooking can be equally random. When it's good, it's really delicious. But now and again, it's simply OK.

Still, Wishbone appeals on many levels. Rock-solid signatures like the Pork Chop of Awe and Wonder ($25), an inches-thick center-cut slab of milk-fed meat from Nicasio's Devil's Gulch Ranch, is juniper-brined, roasted to a golden edge, and plopped atop a mountain of mashed potatoes with a dollop of fruit-whiskey compote to smack some sweetness into the jus.

Co-owners Miriam Donaldson and Josh Norwitt previously operated Humble Pie in Penngrove and Blue Label in Santa Rosa. Both were pleasing but short lived, perhaps because of their obscure locations. This Wishbone is in a well-known space; it's the former home of Three Cooks Cafe, which closed when owner Bobby Mangano retired after 27 years working the stoves.

With a shiny silver wishbone in the front window, the space feels welcoming as soon as you enter. It often smells - and sounds - like sizzling bacon, as it did one night when the menu featured two tiny bacon-wrapped quail paired with strawberry couscous and halved mini artichokes ($24). Too bad the artichokes were tasteless and tough to eat.

The savory bread pudding that comes with the chicken plate ($20) is superb, pillowy and eggy inside with meaty gems of mushroom against a crunchy crust. But the petite half chicken wavered on the edge of undercooked, with little hint of the promised lavender salt.

On the plus side, I could make a main course out of the Brussels sprouts gratin appetizer ($6), since the dish is so rich with creamy white cheddar and toasted pecans.

"Blueballs" - meatballs spiked with Point Reyes blue cheese ($9) - are good, too, alongside house-made ketchup and, unexpectedly, Brussels sprouts; they make a nice snack at the counter with a cold Old Speckled Hen ale ($5.50) or a glass of Hobo Rockpile Zinfandel ($11).

The best starter, though, is the golden trout salad ($9). The snowfall of shredded house-smoked fish is beautifully presented in a crisp romaine boat brimming with crunchy apple and toasted pecans.

For a simple meal, the Farmer's Supper ($20) brings a generous serving of oft-changing charcuterie, cheese, fruit, green salad and a small loaf of homemade milk and honey bread. However, served nearly overflowing on a plate, it didn't look as appealing as it might have.

The kitchen seems most consistent at lunch and brunch, where a flaky biscuit smothered in pork sausage gravy, two eggs and well-crisped potatoes ($12) makes a hearty, satisfying meal.

A monster-tall BLT ($13) also stands out, with thick-crisp house-made black pepper bacon stacked high with tomatoes, greens and house-made mayo on country toast.

In the end, Wishbone remains one of my neighborhood go-tos. If the food falters here and there, the vibe helps fill the gaps. And when a meal ends with a big slab of top-notch, house-baked seasonal fruit pie ($5) or a warm cinnamon roll ($5) slathered with bacon-studded icing, what's not to forgive?

Wishbone

841 Petaluma Blvd. (near Magnolia Avenue), Petaluma; (707) 763-2663. www.wishbonepetaluma.com. Lunch 11 a.m.-2:30 p.m. and dinner 5-9 p.m. Wednesday-Sunday. Beer and wine. Reservations and credit cards accepted. Free parking lot.
