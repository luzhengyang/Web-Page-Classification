McDonald's has a new generational problem: kids

By Lisa Bertagnoli September 08, 2014
 4  5  1 Google +0  13
Crain's illustration / Manuel Martinez, Thinkstock photos

As a girl, Rachel Havreberg occasionally went to McDonald's with her parents. Today, as a mother, she'll take her 7-year-old daughter, Marin, to Chipotle Mexican Grill and neighborhood taco stands, but McDonald's? Never.

“I don't even consider fast food to be real food,” says Ms. Havreberg, 30, who says she stopped eating fast food when she was 15, after reading about factory farming. Not even good-for-you menu additions like apple slices and yogurt change her judgment: McDonald's, she tells her daughter, “is junk and not worth eating.”

Ronald McDonald isn't aging well. More and more millennials like Ms. Havreberg, a sales associate at a toy store in Chicago's Lincoln Square, are shunning the chain, as McDonald's Corp. itself has acknowledged. Now there are signs it is losing the next generation, the Happy Meal cohort.

The slippage helps explain McDonald's declining sales at its U.S. restaurants. It also suggests just how hard it will be for the Oak Brook company to pull out of its slump. McDonald's would get a lift, of course, if working-class moms and dads felt flush enough to treat the family to a meal out. A new hit product would generate traffic, too. But McDonald's is facing a more intractable problem: Raised in a country where ethnic food often is commonplace and bombarded with anti-processed-food messages, kids no longer crave McDonald's like they used to.

“Kids are more sophisticated,” says Mary Chapman, director of product innovation at Chicago-based Technomic Inc., a restaurant research firm. “They're not just looking for the Golden Arches and the toy.”

Families with kids also are declining as a percentage of visitors at McDonald's. By mid-2014, families with a child age 12 or under accounted for 14.6 percent of McDonald's visitors, from 18.6 percent in 2011, according to Technomic research on the composition of customers of the nation's 100 largest restaurant chains. During that same period, all quick-service restaurants had fewer visits from kids and families. The overall decline, though, was less steep, to 12.4 percent from 14.2 percent.

This year, McDonald's lost its perennial first-place position as the chain with the most “kid appeal,” according to Sandelman & Associates Inc., a restaurant research firm in Barrington. For the first time in 25 years, the top spot went to Chick-fil-A Inc., the Atlanta-based chicken sandwich chain that has eight outlets in the Chicago area and 1,800 nationwide and more than $5 billion in annual sales.

'PEOPLE LOVE TO HATE IT'

While the burger giant still is a “dominating force” among families due to its sheer size—it has 14,000 locations in the U.S., more than any other chain except Subway—”McDonald's is a target,” says Bev Cain, a senior vice president at Sandelman. “People love to hate it.”

The chain is trying to adapt to this new consumer. McDonald's recently reorganized marketing to focus on demographic groups—families, millennials and adults—rather than food groups from its menu. It now offers apples and, starting this fall, Cuties clementines, as options to french fries in its Happy Meals. In July, strawberry Go-Gurt yogurt was added. McDonald's also sells a 20-piece Chicken McNuggets box in recognition that “sharing food is a growing trend for families and millennials,” says Brian Irwin, McDonald's USA marketing director.

“We remain focused on listening to all of our customers and evolving our menus to meet their expectations and eating habits,” Mr. Irwin says in an email. McDonald's “takes our responsibility to children and family very seriously, and we offer food choices that fit within a balanced diet.”

And McDonald's can outspend its critics. One of the biggest advertisers in the country, the chain reportedly spent nearly $1 billion on advertising in 2013, a 25 percent increase from 2012's $787.5 million. Its marketing runs the gamut from sponsoring the 2014 Winter Olympic Games and the World Cup to the annual BlogHer conference, which attracts about 3,000 “mommy bloggers” and other online scribes.

(A McDonald's spokeswoman declines to verify the estimates and says that the chain does not comment on advertising strategies.)

Big Mac haters aren't exactly new. Advocacy groups such as the Parents Television Council in Los Angeles have been attacking the company for years for sponsoring kids shows on Saturday morning TV. Likewise, Morgan Spurlock binged on McDonald's food in “Super Size Me” a decade ago. What was different then was McDonald's sales—and share price—kept on rising.

These days, the company clearly is struggling. Globally, sales at outlets open at least 13 months have fallen or have been flat for nine months in a row. After July's comparable sales dropped an unexpected 3.2 percent, Janney Capital Markets analyst Mark Kalinowski in New York predicts the company may post its first full year of declining sales since 2002.

Rachel Havreberg tells daughter Marin that McDonald's food is "junk and not worth eating." Photo: Manuel Martinez

In 2008, during the depths of the Great Recession, McDonald's was one of only two stocks on the Dow Jones Industrial Average to go up in value. This year, as market indexes set record highs, McDonald's stock was down 4.1 percent, to $95.92, on Sept. 5, making it the fourth-worst-performing stock among the Dow Jones 30.

In August, shortly after reporting its disappointing July results, CEO Don Thompson sacrificed his second U.S. chief in two years, bringing in restaurant industry veteran Mike Andres (who started out as a manager of a McDonald's franchise in California more than 30 years ago). And this month, Marlena Peleo-Lazar left as the company's U.S. chief creative officer.

SOCIAL MEDIA SNARK

McDonald's kids-oriented marketing hasn't helped much lately. When the company showed off its updated Ronald McDonald earlier this year, he was mocked widely on social media. Happy—a toothy, sneaker-wearing icon for Happy Meals—similarly drew derision from marketing experts as well as snark when it was introduced in May.

“Anything they do that involves children, they get jumped,” says Scott Hume, founder of BurgerBusiness.com, an online magazine. “There are critics waiting for anything that they do.” As a result, he adds, McDonald's “has been a bit gun shy and is figuring out what it can do without drawing criticism.”

Today's kids are different from their parents and grandparents when they were little. Thanks to Chipotle, Noodles & Co. and other “fast-casual” restaurants that have sprouted over the past decade, many kids have cut their teeth on fare more exotic than burgers and fries. Indeed, in an unscientific Crain's survey of family dining habits, half of 211 respondents say they'd go to Chipotle for a family meal. Potbelly places second with a little more than a third, while McDonald's comes in right behind it.

When Lindsay Pinchuk and her husband take their 4-year-old daughter out for a dinner at a chain restaurant, it's likely to be Potbelly or, for a burger, Epic Burger. “The meat is higher-quality, and it's reflected in the price,” she says.

Though Ms. Pinchuk, 34, founder of Chicago-based Bump Club & Beyond, an event-planning company that connects mothers and mothers-to-be, frequently ate at McDonald's when she was little, she says now, “There's nothing I would eat at McDonald's.”

As for Marin Havreberg, it turns out the second-grader has eaten at McDonald's. This summer, her camp bus stopped there, and she ordered a burger. “It was disgusting,” she says.

McDonald's has to win her over. How? That's what company execs had better figure out—and fast.

This article has been corrected regarding fruit and vegetable options in McDonald's Happy Meals.
