    Wells Fargo Banks on European Real Estate

    Bank Sees an Opening to Capture Market Share

    ENLARGE
    A rendering of London Wall Place, a tower being built by Brookfield Office Properties and financed with a construction loan from Wells Fargo. Brookfield Office Properties/Oxford Properties
    By
    Sarah Krouse,
    Eliot Brown and
    • Eliot Brown
      The Wall Street Journal
      CANCEL
    • BiographyEliot Brown
    • Eliot.Brown@wsj.com
    • @eliotwb
    Emily Glazer
    • Emily Glazer
      The Wall Street Journal
      CANCEL
    • BiographyEmily Glazer
    • Emily.Glazer@wsj.com
    • @EmilyGlazerWSJ

    Oct. 21, 2014 4:37 p.m. ET
    3 COMMENTS

    Wells Fargo & Co., the largest U.S. commercial-property lender, is pushing deeper into Europe amid worries about the strength of the U.K. market and as eurozone countries slide toward recession.

    Some of the region’s big lenders pulled out in the wake of the 2008 financial crisis and the recovery of Europe’s property sector has been uneven since then. But the area has shown just enough strength to tempt U.S. lenders and investors eager to spread their reach.

    “We want to be one of the go-to providers” in Britain, said Max Sinclair, head of U.K. commercial real estate at Wells Fargo. Its goal is for clients ranging from developers to real-estate investment trusts and sovereign-wealth funds “to think of us in their top three organizations to have a chat with when they have a potential transaction,” he said.

    Executives at San Francisco-based Wells declined to provide growth targets for its U.K. expansion, but said the bank is looking at Ireland and continental Europe as well.

    ENLARGE

    The timing is tricky. Weak economic data in recent weeks have fueled worries about growth in Germany and France. What’s more, the U.K. property market, Wells Fargo’s main focus so far, has been heating up for several years and there is concern among some brokers that it might be poised for a pullback.

    Clarence Dixon, head of loan servicing at CBRE, said the U.K. had become increasingly competitive in the past year. Lenders originated or refinanced about £30 billion ($48 billion) in U.K. commercial real-estate loans last year, up from £25.5 billion in 2012 and £15.1 billion in 2009, according to De Montfort University research.

    Wells Fargo executives acknowledged there might be a “pause for breath” in the U.K. market in the next year or two, and that they are taking a cautious approach to new deals there. While some private-equity firms are hunting for distressed assets, Wells Fargo is focusing on making loans to U.S. and U.K. clients for office buildings, retail properties and warehouses, and are considering moving into student and multifamily housing, they said.

    For Wells Fargo, Europe is attractive partly because there are fewer opportunities at home and partly because Europe’s sluggish economy and dearth of competitors makes it relatively easy to grab market share and grow quickly.

    The bank already was among the largest holders of U.S. commercial real-estate debt heading into the downturn, and became by far the dominant lender in the sector in 2008 when it acquired Wachovia Corp. As of the second quarter, Wells Fargo reported commercial real-estate loan holdings of $122 billion, well ahead of the second-largest firm, J.P. Morgan Chase & Co., which reported $79 billion.

    At the same time, in the U.K. and continental Europe, some of the biggest lenders before the downturn either have failed or remain wary of the sector. Gone are onetime giants like now-defunct Lehman Brothers Holdings Inc. and Anglo Irish Banks of Ireland, as well as Commerzbank AG of Germany, which is winding down its commercial real-estate financing business.

    In all, North American banks accounted for only 5% of all commercial-property loan originations in the U.K. in 2013, according to De Montfort University research.

    The retreat has opened the door for Wells Fargo.

    The bank made its first big foray into the U.K. last year when it paid £4 billion to buy property loans from Commerzbank’s real-estate unit, Hypothekenbank Frankfurt AG, and hired the bank’s commercial real-estate team.

    Wells Fargo sold off the nonperforming loans, which represented about a third of the portfolio, to private-equity firm Lone Star Funds, and then boosted its holdings back to about $6 billion by making loans in the U.K.

    Executives said the European push is a major initiative for Wells Fargo’s commercial-real estate team, which accounts for about 8% of the bank’s total assets. Wells Fargo has about 950 employees across its business lines in London and about 35 focused solely on property, up from fewer than 10 before it bought the Commerzbank portfolio last year.

    Wells isn’t the only big U.S. lender looking to Europe.

    General Electric Corp.’s GE Capital boosted its European commercial-property debt portfolio to $4.7 billion at the end of June, from $3.1 billion a year earlier. The division hopes to increase that to as much as $8 billion in three years, said Ellen Brunsberg, who oversees European commercial real estate for GE Capital.

    J.P. Morgan, meanwhile, was the runner-up to Wells Fargo for the Commerzbank portfolio in the U.K. Later it joined with Lone Star to buy a €4.5 billion ($5.6 billion) portfolio of Commerzbank commercial-property loans in Spain and Portugal named Project Octopus.

    The largest U.S. bank by assets wants to keep growing in Europe by making and buying loans, people familiar with its strategy said.

    Wells Fargo in June provided £130 million to Brookfield Property Partners, one of the largest owners of office space in the U.S., for its London Wall office development, according to people familiar with the deal. The bank also recently bid on a loan for another Brookfield project, a new east London office-tower development known as Principal Place that is slated to house a new U.K. office for Amazon.com Inc., according to people familiar with the project.

    Property advisers said buying the Hypothekenbank loans, and hiring its U.K. team, gave Wells Fargo credibility in the market.

    The cachet helped it land a piece of a Canary Wharf Group deal, becoming one of four banks that provided £600 million in financing to the developer of the eponymous financial office hub in East London.

    “In London, we operate in quite a tightly knit village, and in order to be a successful lender you need to be part of that fraternity,” said William Newsom, senior director at property advisory firm Savills.
