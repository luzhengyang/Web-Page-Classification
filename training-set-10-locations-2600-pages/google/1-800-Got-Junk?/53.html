    1-800-GOT-JUNK: You got it, they'll take it

    Jul. 4, 2011
     
    • Print
    • A
    • A
    Purchase Image
    Mickey Saltzman of 1-800-GOT-JUNK? emerges from the basement of a house in Essex Junction with a chair to be hauled away. / DAN D’AMBROSIO, Free Press
    Written by
    Dan D’Ambrosio, Free Press Staff Writer

    • Filed Under
    • Business
    • BUSINESS03
    ADVERTISEMENT

    Peter Belden is waiting for John Wagner and Mickey Saltzman when they pull up in their 1-800-GOT-JUNK? truck to the home in Essex Junction where Belden’s late parents lived for some 15 years.

    Belden and his brother have sold the house after renting it for a month as a favor to a friend. Belden is here to get the house ready for a closing July 11.

    Wagner, manager of the Burlington franchise for the junk removal company, shakes Belden’s hand and begins explaining the 1-800-GOT-JUNK? approach. He’s wearing a navy polo shirt with the company logo on the chest, blue Dickies work pants and work boots. Saltzman is wearing the same uniform. The pair has arrived at 9:45 a.m., the time Wagner told Belden he would be there in a phone call a half-hour earlier.

    “We’ll do all the lifting, loading and sorting for you and clean up afterwards,” Wagner says. “We’ll try to divert everything we can from the landfill and back to the community through repurposing and recycling.”

    After a walk through the house, with Belden pointing out what stays and what goes as he dodges piles of clothing left behind by the renter, Wagner gives Belden what he calls a “three-point estimate” — high, low and in the middle — based on how much of the truck his junk is going to fill. He estimates 7/8 to 1 1/8 of a truck, which will cost Belden between $585 and $777.

    Belden was sold after the recycling and repurposing pitch.

    “That’s awesome, that’s why I picked you guys,” he says.

    Later, Belden said he has seen the 1-800-GOT-JUNK? trucks “all over.” He and his brother had already emptied the house of all the “memorable” items, and the furniture they wanted. Now there were odds and ends, such as a bookcase without books and a massive, home-built shelving unit at least eight feet long that will take three 1-800-GOT-JUNK? guys to leverage out of the basement. All of it goes.

    “I’m certain that the cost for my time and the cost to do everything would be as much if not more than the cost of this,” Belden says. “It matches the culture of Vermont with the recycling. That’s why I liked them the most. It makes a lot of sense for anybody doing this stuff to consider them.”

    Sometimes the recycling is not so easy. Saltzman recalls a 600-pound, five-foot-tall tractor tire he and a co-worker picked up at a day care center that no longer wanted it.
    “We ended up having to push it into the truck,” Saltzman says. “It took us about an hour. We would push it up and it would roll back down.”

    Saltzman and his partner finally got the giant tire to the Chittenden County Solid Waste District, where it was plucked from their truck with a big crane.

    “We didn’t want to have to mess with it again,” Saltzman says.

    Wagner once recycled a pole barn in East Montpelier, using a chain saw to cut it down.

    “It was quite a feat,” he says. “They told me it was 15 feet by 15 feet. When we got there, it was 30 feet by 15 feet with a boat underneath it and a bunch of flooring. It had collapsed partially. Took us three hours. We charge a separate fee for something like that. It’s a case-by-case scenario.”

    Wagner says he and his crews have worked on at least 60 jobs related to this year’s record-breaking flooding. Those are tough jobs, not like at the Belden household, where everything is basically neat and tidy.

    “You got the pretty version of the work today; it’s not always this nice,” Wagner says. “Imagine a swampy basement with moldy drywall that was just put in to build a playroom for the kids. The flood ruined everything and then we’re shoveling and scooping this stuff out, white as ghosts with drywall chalk all over us.”

    Wagner and Saltzman can also find themselves in emotionally explosive situations. A nasty divorce, for example, which leaves one party cleaning the house out of the other party’s possessions and vowing to send the other party the bill.

    “You have to play along. ‘Oh, sorry to hear she treated you that way.’ You kind of got to side with somebody. You don’t want to make them angry,” Wagner says. “We’re not saying, ‘Let’s be fake with the customer.’ We’re genuine people. We have genuine feelings towards the situation we deal with, but we have no impact. We have nothing to say on a situation where you’re cussing your ex-husband out, and his daughter, and throwing out everything nice you can imagine.”

    After hauling out several loads of relatively light and portable junk, Wagner and Saltzman now confront the massive shelving unit.

    “This thing is big man,” Saltzman says.

    “I’m good if you’re good,” Wagner replies.

    The two men tip the unit. They decide to wait for a third man to arrive.

    Aaron Fastman, 37, launched the Burlington franchise of 1-800-GOT-JUNK? in the fall of 2006 after winning a grueling two-week-long interview process in Vancouver, British Columbia, beating out three other people vying for the last franchise in North America, covering Vermont and Plattsburgh, N.Y., north to the Canadian border.

    “They liked that I was young, I was driven, and I had the capital,” Fastman says.

    It took $100,000 to nail down the franchise. Fastman borrowed $70,000 from two of his father’s business partners, and had the other $30,000 himself after five years of working two jobs. He said he hopes to finish repaying the $70,000 he borrowed this year, if he has a good summer.

    Brian Scudamore founded 1-800-GOT-JUNK? in 1989 in Vancouver at the age of 18, and built it into a business with about 1,000 trucks on the road in 180 locations in Canada, the United States and Australia. The franchise is moving into the United Kingdom and Europe, and Scudamore also has his eye on South America.

    “I already have my name on some of those lists,” Fastman says.

    When he was 20 years old, Fastman, who grew up in Woodstock, N.Y., had an experience that made him uniquely suited to the ups and downs of starting and running a business. He was diagnosed with Hodgkin’s disease and endured six months of chemotherapy and a month of radiation therapy to eliminate tumors in the lymph glands in his neck and chest.

    Today, Fastman is still in remission, and has a 4-year-old son Ezra, sharing custody with his ex-wife.

    “Fifteen years ago they were telling me, ‘There’s an 80 percent chance you’re going to die,’” Fastman said. “Now I have a son. That feeling of loving life, I bring that into this business. We’re doing a good thing for the planet and I believe in that. I’m trying to make some money also on the top.”
