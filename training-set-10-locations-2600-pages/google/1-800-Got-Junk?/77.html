Former salesman finds happiness-and income-in other people's junk

Carl Schilling wants your junk.

With three Isuzu trucks and a handful of uniformed, friendly employees, Schilling is out to clean up as the area franchisee for 1-800-GOT-JUNK?

Schilling invested about $150,000, including two trucks at $45,000 each, and the money to buy the rights to five 125,000-population territories encompassing an area from Albany to Saratoga. He pays an 8 percent royalty annually to the franchiser.

The average 1-800-GOT-JUNK? franchise investor spends about $75,000 to $90,000 to get started.

"I started a little more aggressive than some," Schilling said.

Schilling began operating in September 2005. In March he bought another truck and a sixth territory, allowing him to pick up junk in Schenectady.

Happy with 96 percent

Schilling, a 20-year veteran of construction-equipment sales, most recently worked for Albany's Contractor Sales Co. Inc. He said that although he enjoyed his career, he'd always wanted to be his own boss. He had an opportunity about 10 years ago to get into a cell-phone business, but he had young children and his sales career was going well, so he decided not to take the risk.

But last year his self-imposed deadline to have a business by age 40 was coming up. His wife, Diane Schilling, a state attorney and East Greenbush town justice, was doing well in her career, so he felt it was the right time to strike out on his own.

He chose 1-800-GOT-JUNK? because he liked the system.

The franchise was started by Vancouver, British Columbia, entrepreneur Brian Scudamore in 1989 as a student-run business. The company began franchising in 1998 and now has 250 franchisees. The company was No. 78 in Entrepreneur magazine's list of 100 fast-growing franchises in 2006. Revenue is expects to reach 100 million by the end of the year.

Schilling set his own revenue goal at $470,000 for the year. Halfway through, he reached $216,000 and is certain he'll come very close to the goal.

"I'm trying to get to 96 percent," he said. "A lot of my high school teachers would have been happy with Carl getting a 96 percent, so I guess I'll be happy."

We accept bmws

Except for a couple of computers, a phone and one of two deer heads on the wall, Schilling's office on Hoosick Street in Troy was furnished with items from other offices. He got his desk from a doctor who didn't want it anymore, copiers from an insurance company that was moving, and sofas, chairs and two Halloween skeletons from other offices and homes.

He even has a BMW--with 165,000 miles on it--that he took as payment for cleaning out a basement.

Schilling said there are a few key points to the 1-800-GOT-JUNK? service.

The company almost always makes pickups within two hours of the scheduled time. Schilling will send a crew out to estimate the cost of a job and then give the customer a quote. Costs are based on how much of the 400 cubic feet of truck space a load will take. Each truck can carry up to three tons. The minimum cost is $98 for an eighth of a truckload. Most jobs fill about three-quarters of a truck, Schilling said.

The crews who remove the junk are uniformed and professional-looking. Workers are selected for their ability to be personable, Schilling said.

"I can do all the advertising I want to do to get the people calling and making appointments; but if our guys don't know what they are doing, aren't friendly and aren't personable and aren't dressed the right way, that could kill me," he said.

Selling the service

To increase profitability, Schilling keeps as much junk as possible out of the landfills to avoid paying tipping fees. Items that can be recycled, are. Some throwaways, like office furniture or old car seats--there is a market for them--are donated to charities.

To cut fuel costs, Schillinger has cut deals with parking lot owners around the region to pre-position trucks. Not only does it save money driving to busy areas, but the trucks are free advertising, he said.

Some 1-800-GOT-JUNK? franchisees use eBay to sell items they clean out of basements and attics. Schilling said he is not ready to do that, yet.

He is considering installing GPS systems to track where his trucks are so he can route them more effectively. About 60 percent of his business comes from residential customers; 40 percent is from cleaning out offices or other businesses, Schillinger said.

He gets a good bit of residential business from real estate agents eager to clean out houses before closings. Another large chunk of residential business has come from estate settlements, when a family needs to clean 40 years of accumulated items out of a basement.

There's plenty of money to be made in trash, but Schillinger doesn't consider the major waste haulers, or the roll-on/roll-off dumpster companies to be direct competition.

Big haulers like County Waste & Recycling Service Inc. are looking for a daily or weekly trash pickup. And dumpster companies require customers to fill the dumpster themselves, instead of sending a crew, Schillinger said.

A Saratoga company, 1-888-CHUCKIT, offers a service based on the 1-800-GOT-JUNK? model.

Douglas Dockendorf, the owner, operates five trucks and 10 employees.

Dockendorf, who says he got the idea from 1-800-GOT-JUNK?, started his service 18 months ago. He works the entire Capital Region and said he is expanding.

Dockendorf said CHUCKIT donates items to more than 60 charities. He sends out an e-mail each week to the participants telling them what is available. The company delivers the used goods, he said.

Nationally, several companies have borrowed GOT-JUNK? model. A company called Trashbusters, with trucks displaying a sign proclaiming "Get 1-800 RID OF IT" operates in Seattle and in 1-800-GOT-JUNK?'s birthplace of Vancouver.

Schilling said his goal now is to shift the day-to-day operations to a newly hired manager. Schilling will be out selling the service. That means talking to Realtors about what GOT-JUNK? can do, and making contacts in the business community, Schilling said.

"There is more business in this area that we are not getting that is available to us. I need to let everybody know that we are here, and we haven't done that yet."
