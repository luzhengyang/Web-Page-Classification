Domino's Pizza targeted after abuse at dairy farm

Eric D. Lawrence, Detroit Free Press 3:39 p.m. EDT September 17, 2014

An animal welfare group is targeting Ann Arbor-based Domino's Pizza and other chains with its release of a video showing workers abusing cows at a farm that supplies milk for pizza cheese.

The undercover video, titled "Slice of Cruelty" and taken at a dairy farm in Lake Arthur, N.M., shows workers punching, kicking and whipping cows and shocking their genitals with electric prods, tossing calves into truck beds, using heaving equipment to force sick animals into confined spots and committing other types of abuse.

"This is certainly some of the most shocking abuse that we've ever documented," said Matt Rice, director of investigations for Los Angeles-based Mercy for Animals.

The group specifically called on Domino's to take action.

"Mercy for Animals is urging Domino's Pizza, one of the largest pizza companies in the world, to help end egregious animal abuse in the dairy industry by requiring all of its cheese suppliers to implement meaningful animal welfare policies," the group said in an e-mail.

Tim McIntyre, vice president of communications for Domino's, called the case isolated.

"No act of cruelty can ever be condoned. Ever. What we do know is that this is not an issue with our cheese supplier it was an isolated case of sadistic acts by employees at a single dairy farm in southern New Mexico. That farmer, who is very likely reeling from this, has terminated the employees, turned their information over to law enforcement and has closed his operations after moving his cows to other farms (according to the Associated Press)," McIntyre said in an e-mail.

McIntyre said the group should be thanked for bringing the issue to light because "there is no room for this anywhere in the food industry," but cautioned against a wider interpretation.

"America's individual family dairy farms 47,000 of them are being painted in a horrible light due to the horrendous acts of a small group of individuals. That's not fair to the hardworking farmers across America," McIntyre said.

An investigator for the group was hired at Winchester Dairy and documented what he witnessed from Aug. 6 through Sept. 5.

The New Mexico Livestock Board, which has oversight responsibility for the dairy farm, has launched an investigation of the operation, according to the Associated Press.

"We are investigating it very aggressively. The district attorney is on board and everybody is working hard to make sure we do this right," Shawn Davis, an area supervisor with the livestock board, told the AP.

The dairy said in a statement to the Associated Press that it has fired all employees and referred the abusive workers to law enforcement for further review following its own internal investigation.

The dairy also halted milking operations, stopped shipments to all vendors and dispersed thousands of cows to other dairies with strong track records in animal welfare, the AP said.

"We remain committed to the ethical and responsible treatment of the animals and have learned from this incident," the dairy told the AP.
